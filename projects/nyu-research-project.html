Public Network + AI in Cybersecurity

Context

This research project focused on exploring the cybersecurity risks posed by public networks and AI-powered threats to Air Traffic Control (ATC) systems. The goal was to assess real-world vulnerabilities and investigate how malicious actors could exploit these systems, potentially causing serious disruptions to transportation safety and efficiency.Our process included gathering background research, building a threat model, analyzing past incidents (such as the 2015 Warsaw hack), and proposing mitigation strategies.

More text about the project.

Content

Our research covered key areas including:

  • How public Wi-Fi and unsecured networks near airports can be entry points for attacks
  • The role of AI-enhanced attacks, such as AI-assisted phishing or automated intrusion
  • Real-world examples of malware in aviation systems (e.g., flight delays due to compromised software)
  • A threat model simulating how a hacker could move from a public access point to critical ATC systems
  • A risk assessment table
  • Proposed solutions, such as isolating critical systems, using firewalls/encryption, and deploying AI for real-time threat detection

Challenges

One major challenge was understanding technical systems like ATC architecture and how data flows between components. We had to simplify complex ideas for a broader audience. Another difficulty was designing the threat model from a hacker’s perspective without going into unethical or overly technical territory. This required balancing depth with clarity.We also had to stay updated with fast-changing AI tools that could be used for both defense and attack—making the risk landscape more complex.

Takeaways

  • Public networks may seem harmless, but when linked to insecure systems, they become a serious threat.
  • AI can accelerate both good and bad actors, which means our defenses need to evolve faster too.
  • Planning and modeling before writing the paper helped keep our arguments strong and well-structured.

Next Steps

In the future, I’d like to expand this research into a interactive website where users can simulate threats and responses.
